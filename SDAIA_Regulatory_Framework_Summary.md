**SDAIA REGULATORY FRAMEWORK**

A Comprehensive Summary Report

**Saudi Data & Artificial Intelligence Authority**

_Data Governance | AI Ethics | Personal Data Protection | Cross-Border Transfers | Emerging AI Law_

# 1\. Overview & Institutional Mandate

The Saudi Data and Artificial Intelligence Authority (SDAIA) is the Kingdom's primary regulatory and policy-setting body for data and AI. Established by Royal Decree in August 2019 and reporting directly to the Prime Minister, SDAIA holds a dual mandate: driving national digital transformation, and regulating the data and AI environment through legislation, standards, guidance, and enforcement.

SDAIA's regulatory authority is underpinned by Council of Ministers Resolution No. 292 (1441 AH), subsequently amended by Resolution No. 195 (1444 AH). Its legislative arm, the National Data Management Office (NDMO), serves as the national regulator and reference body for all matters relating to data governance and personal data protection across the Kingdom.

**Regulatory Guiding Principle**

Data produced, received, or dealt with by government and private entities is one of the most important national assets that contribute to improving performance, productivity, and the provision of public services. Saudi Arabia aims to implement global best practices for data management and governance, protect personal data, and maximize the value of data as an economic resource that fosters innovation and national competitiveness.

# 2\. Regulatory Architecture

SDAIA's regulatory framework is structured across four interconnected layers: foundational legislation, institutional governance, sector-specific instruments, and operational guidelines. Together they form a multi-tier system designed to be both robust and agile.

| **Layer** | **Instruments** | **Administered By** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Foundational Law** | Personal Data Protection Law (PDPL); Royal Decree M/19 (2021), amended M/148 (2023); Anti-Cyber Crime Law; e-Commerce Law; Telecom & IT Law | SDAIA / NDMO |
| **National Data Governance** | National Data Governance Interim Regulations; Data Management & Personal Data Protection Standards; Data Classification Policy | NDMO |
| **AI-Specific Instruments** | AI Ethics Principles (2023); AI Adoption Framework (2024); Generative AI Guidelines (2024); Deepfakes Guidelines (2024); Draft Global AI Hub Law (2025) | SDAIA / NCAI |

# 3\. Personal Data Protection Law (PDPL)

The PDPL is the cornerstone of Saudi Arabia's data protection framework - the Kingdom's first comprehensive federal privacy legislation. Issued by Royal Decree No. M/19 on September 16, 2021, and amended by Royal Decree No. M/148 on March 27, 2023, the law came into force on September 14, 2023 with a one-year grace period, becoming fully enforceable on September 14, 2024.

## 3.1 Scope & Extraterritorial Reach

The PDPL applies to any organization - local or foreign - processing the personal data of individuals within Saudi Arabia, regardless of where that processing takes place. Its extraterritorial scope is broader than that of the EU GDPR. All organizations handling Saudi residents' data must comply, including international businesses without a physical presence in the Kingdom.

## 3.2 Six Foundational Principles

| **Purpose Limitation** | Personal data may only be collected for specific, clearly defined, and legitimate purposes. |
| --- | --- |
| **Data Minimization** | Only the minimum personal data necessary for the stated purpose may be collected. |
| **Accuracy** | Controllers must ensure data remains accurate and up to date. |
| **Storage Limitation** | Data must not be retained longer than necessary for the processing purpose. |
| **Security** | Appropriate technical and organizational measures must be applied to protect personal data. |
| **Accountability** | Controllers are responsible for demonstrating compliance with all PDPL obligations. |

## 3.3 Key Obligations for Organizations

- Obtain valid, explicit consent for personal data processing - including automated decision-making.
- Register on the National Data Governance Platform if processing sensitive data (health, genetic, biometric, racial/ethnic, religious, security, or criminal data).
- Appoint a Data Protection Officer (DPO) where required by SDAIA rules.
- Maintain Records of Processing Activities (RoPAs).
- Conduct Data Protection Impact Assessments (DPIAs) for high-risk processing activities.
- Notify SDAIA and affected individuals of personal data breaches in a timely manner.
- Honor data subject rights: access, rectification, erasure, and restriction of processing.

## 3.4 Sensitive Personal Data

The PDPL establishes a heightened category of sensitive personal data, defined to include: racial or ethnic origin; religious, intellectual, or political belief; security/criminal conviction data; biometric or genetic data used for identification; health data; and data indicating parentage is unknown. Processing of sensitive data carries additional obligations and restrictions.

**Enforcement Note**

As of September 2024, SDAIA actively enforces PDPL compliance through investigations, fines, and regulatory actions for data breaches and consent violations. Privacy-related litigation is still in its early stages; administrative enforcement actions have been the dominant regulatory mechanism to date.

# 4\. National Data Governance Framework

The NDMO - SDAIA's legislative arm - has developed a comprehensive national data governance framework covering both government and private sector data. This framework establishes classification, management, and sharing standards that underpin the Kingdom's entire data economy.

## 4.1 Data Classification Levels

The National Data Governance Interim Regulations establish four tiers of data classification, governing how data may be processed and used in AI training and deployment:

| **Public** | Freely shareable; no access restrictions. |
| --- | --- |
| **Internal** | For use within the relevant organization; limited external sharing. |
| **Restricted** | Requires authorization for access; sharing subject to controls. |
| **Confidential** | Highest protection tier; includes nationally sensitive and personally sensitive data. |

## 4.2 National Data Governance Platform

Controllers required to register under PDPL - particularly those processing sensitive data - must register on the National Data Governance Platform maintained by SDAIA. The Platform provides the infrastructure for controller registration, data breach notifications, and regulatory monitoring.

## 4.3 Data Sovereignty Policy

In 2024, SDAIA released a Draft Data Sovereignty Public Policy built on four core principles: (1) data as a national strategic asset; (2) prioritizing national security in data governance; (3) enabling data-driven innovation while protecting individual rights; and (4) fostering international data cooperation under conditions of trust. This policy underpins all cross-border data transfer regulations.

# 5\. Cross-Border Data Transfer Regime

Cross-border data transfers are a key focus area within SDAIA's framework, balancing openness for international business with stringent safeguards for national security and data sovereignty.

## 5.1 Transfer Mechanisms

The Regulation on Personal Data Transfer Outside the Kingdom (updated August 2024, supplemented by additional guidance in February 2025) provides three lawful bases for international personal data transfers:

| **Adequacy Decision** | Transfer to countries assessed by SDAIA as providing an adequate level of data protection. SDAIA conducts formal adequacy assessments of recipient jurisdictions. |
| --- | --- |
| **Standard Contractual Clauses (SCCs)** | SDAIA has issued four official SCC templates covering: Controller-to-Controller; Controller-to-Processor; Processor-to-Processor; and Processor-to-Controller transfers. |
| **Binding Corporate Rules (BCRs)** | Equivalent safeguards approved by SDAIA, applicable for multinational groups transferring data internally across jurisdictions. |

## 5.2 Risk Assessment Requirements

Organizations must conduct a formal risk assessment prior to transferring personal data outside the Kingdom. SDAIA's Risk Assessment Guideline (February 2025) provides the structured methodology for this assessment, which must consider national security implications, the data classification level, and the adequacy of protections in the destination jurisdiction.

The PDPL mandates that strategically or nationally sensitive data be stored within the Kingdom - reinforcing data localization requirements for the most critical data assets.

## 5.3 Secondary Use of Data - Emerging Regulations

In April 2025, SDAIA launched public consultations on secondary-use-of-data rules, which will govern responsible data sharing between public entities and the private sector. These rules are expected to unlock significant public sector data for innovation while embedding controls for privacy and national interest protection.

# 6\. AI-Specific Regulatory Instruments

While Saudi Arabia's AI-specific instruments currently function primarily as non-binding guidance, they carry significant regulatory weight - particularly for government entities, which are often required to follow them through internal directives and SDAIA oversight. The trend is clearly toward increasing formalization and, ultimately, dedicated AI legislation.

## 6.1 AI Ethics Principles (2023)

Published initially in draft form in September 2023 and finalized in 2023, the AI Ethics Principles establish the overarching ethical framework for AI development and deployment in the Kingdom. They apply during all stages of the AI system development lifecycle and cover:

- Fairness - AI systems must be free from discrimination and treat all individuals equitably.
- Transparency - AI processes, decisions, and limitations must be explainable and disclosed.
- Accountability - Clear responsibility must exist for AI outcomes and their consequences.
- Inclusivity - AI must be designed to serve the broadest possible range of individuals and communities.
- Privacy and Security - AI systems must embed data protection and cybersecurity safeguards by design.
- Reliability - AI systems must be robust, accurate, and subject to ongoing human oversight.

## 6.2 AI Adoption Framework (September 2024)

The AI Adoption Framework provides a comprehensive, structured roadmap for integrating AI across all sectors of the economy. It defines four maturity levels for organizational AI readiness, and identifies four key enablers that organizations must develop:

| **Data** | Organizations must establish data governance, quality controls, and responsible data management practices as the foundation for AI. |
| --- | --- |
| **Technology** | Adequate compute infrastructure, AI tools, and digital platforms must be in place to support AI deployment. |
| **Human Capabilities** | Workforce skills, training programs, and AI literacy must be developed to drive and govern AI adoption. |
| **Responsible Use** | Internal AI governance offices, ethical review processes, and alignment with the AI Ethics Principles must be embedded. |

In September 2024, SDAIA also published the National AI Index (NAII), which measures the AI readiness of government entities and provides tailored recommendations to drive adoption and compliance.

## 6.3 Generative AI Guidelines (January 2024)

Responding to the rapid rise of large language models (LLMs) and generative AI systems, SDAIA issued two versions of its Generative AI Guidelines in January 2024 - one for government employees, and one for the general public. These guidelines address:

- Content authenticity requirements - Organizations must ensure AI-generated content is clearly identified.
- Watermarking - AI-generated content must carry verifiable watermarks to prevent fraud and misinformation.
- Governance of generative AI outputs - Organizations must implement detection, oversight, and audit mechanisms.
- Risks of hallucinations, bias, and synthetic content - Practical guidance on mitigating these risks in operational environments.
- Challenges and considerations for common scenarios - Sector-specific guidance for government use cases.

## 6.4 Deepfakes Guidelines (2024)

The Deepfakes Guidelines specifically address the growing threat of synthetic media, mandating clear disclosure and watermarking for all AI-generated audio-visual content. Organizations must put in place controls to prevent the use of deepfake technology for fraud, identity theft, or the spread of disinformation.

## 6.5 Automated Decision-Making

The PDPL requires explicit consent for any processing of personal data carried out through automated means where decisions are made solely based on such automated processing. This provision directly governs AI systems used in credit scoring, recruitment screening, medical diagnosis, and other high-impact automated decisions.

**Regulatory Trend**

Current AI-specific instruments function as influential non-binding guidance. However, the regulatory direction is firmly toward formalization. The Draft Global AI Hub Law of 2025 - and the anticipated dedicated AI law - represent the transition from guidance toward binding 'hard law' for AI infrastructure, cross-border data governance, and AI provider obligations.

# 7\. Draft Global AI Hub Law (2025)

The Draft Global AI Hub Law, which underwent public consultation in mid-2025, represents a landmark development in Saudi Arabia's regulatory trajectory. It introduces a revolutionary legal framework designed to make the Kingdom a global destination for sovereign compute and international data hosting.

## 7.1 Virtual Hubs and Private Hubs

The law introduces two new legal constructs: Virtual Hubs (allowing foreign states and multinational corporations to operate under their own legal frameworks for certain data activities while physically located in Saudi Arabia) and Private Hubs (dedicated data processing environments for specific entities or sectors). This 'data embassy' model is expected to trigger a wave of bilateral data treaties and significantly expand the Kingdom's appeal as an international data processing center.

## 7.2 Strategic Linkage to Megaprojects

The Draft Global AI Hub Law is closely linked to the development of NEOM and other megaprojects, where AI is envisaged as the operating system of smart cities - managing autonomous transport, energy grids, public services, and urban infrastructure. These cities will serve as live regulatory testbeds for AI applications, informing future law and policy.

## 7.3 Anticipated Dedicated AI Law

Beyond the AI Hub Law, SDAIA has signaled the development of a comprehensive dedicated AI law. Observers expect this legislation to introduce: risk-based classification of AI systems; mandatory registration and audit duties for high-risk AI providers; liability frameworks for AI-related harms; and a formal regulatory sandbox for testing high-risk AI applications under close supervisory oversight. This will position Saudi Arabia as a regional AI innovation hub with a clear, internationally recognized legal framework.

# 8\. Sectoral Regulatory Integration

Beyond SDAIA, sector-specific regulators integrate AI and data provisions within their existing mandates, aligned with SDAIA's ethics framework and national cybersecurity standards. Key sectoral regulators include:

| **SAMA (Saudi Central Bank)** | Governs AI and data use in financial services, banking, insurance, and fintech - including AI-driven credit decisions, algorithmic trading, and data security requirements for financial institutions. |
| --- | --- |
| **SFDA (Saudi Food & Drug Authority)** | Regulates AI applications in healthcare, medical devices, and diagnostics - including AI-powered diagnostic tools and digital therapeutics. |
| **DGA (Digital Government Authority)** | Oversees AI adoption in public administration and e-government services, ensuring alignment with SDAIA's AI Adoption Framework. |
| **NCA (National Cybersecurity Authority)** | Issues cybersecurity standards applicable to AI systems, including the licensing framework for Managed Security Operations Centre (MSOC) services introduced in 2024. |
| **CITC (Communications & IT Commission)** | Regulates telecommunications and digital platforms, including AI-driven content moderation and platform accountability. |

Regulatory sandboxes - particularly in finance (SAMA FinTech Lab) and telecommunications - allow the testing of high-risk AI applications under close supervisory oversight before broader market release, reflecting an agile, innovation-friendly approach to regulation.

# 10\. Compliance Implications for Organizations

Organizations operating in Saudi Arabia - or processing data of Saudi residents - must navigate a multi-layered compliance environment. The following are the key priority areas:

**PDPL Compliance**

- Audit current data processing activities against PDPL obligations and the six foundational principles.
- Establish or review legal basis for all data processing (consent, legitimate interest, contract, legal obligation).
- Register on the National Data Governance Platform if processing sensitive personal data.
- Appoint a DPO where required; ensure the DPO has adequate authority and resources.
- Implement breach notification procedures aligned with SDAIA reporting requirements.

**Cross-Border Data Transfers**

- Map all outbound data flows and assess their compliance with the Transfer Regulations.
- Implement appropriate SCCs or BCRs using SDAIA-issued templates.
- Conduct formal Risk Assessments for all cross-border transfers, documented in accordance with the February 2025 guideline.
- Ensure sensitive data and nationally significant data is stored within the Kingdom.

**AI Governance**

- Align AI system development and deployment with the AI Ethics Principles - embed fairness, transparency, and accountability from design stage.
- Use the AI Adoption Framework to assess organizational AI maturity and build a structured roadmap toward responsible adoption.
- Apply the Generative AI Guidelines for all government and public-facing AI deployments.
- Establish internal AI governance offices and human oversight mechanisms for automated decision-making.
- Monitor development of the dedicated AI Law and prepare for risk-based classification and registration requirements.

# 11\. Conclusion

SDAIA's regulatory framework is one of the most rapidly evolving and comprehensive in the Middle East region. Built on the foundation of the PDPL and the national data governance regime, it is being actively extended through AI-specific guidelines, cross-border transfer regulations, and the forthcoming Global AI Hub Law and dedicated AI legislation.

The framework reflects a deliberate philosophy: create a trusted, innovation-enabling environment that attracts global investment and positions Saudi Arabia as a sovereign AI hub - while maintaining firm national control over data, protecting individual rights, and embedding ethical principles into the AI development lifecycle.

For organizations operating in or entering the Saudi market, proactive compliance is not only a legal necessity but a strategic advantage: those that invest in PDPL readiness, AI governance, and data sovereignty planning will be best positioned to grow within the Kingdom's expanding data and AI economy.
