# 1\. Introduction

Saudi Arabia is undergoing one of the most transformative economic reforms in its history. Anchored by Vision 2030, the Kingdom has set ambitious targets to diversify its economy away from oil revenues, attract substantial foreign direct investment, and position itself as a leading global business hub.

Foreign direct investment into Saudi Arabia has responded strongly to these reforms. Total FDI reached approximately USD 215 billion between 2017 and 2023, reflecting a 61% increase over that period. The United States alone had invested approximately USD 202.3 billion by early 2025, with around 200 regional headquarters established by American companies. (Sources: MISA Annual Investment Report 2024 / UNCTAD World Investment Report 2024. All figures are approximate and subject to revision; the USD 202.3 billion US investment figure should be independently verified against MISA's official FDI statistics or SAMA's Balance of Payments data prior to publication.)

The most significant recent milestone is the enactment of a new Investment Law pursuant to Royal Decree No. M/19, published on 11 August 2024 and entering into force on 12 February 2025. This law replaces the Foreign Investment Law of 2000 and represents the most comprehensive overhaul of the Kingdom's investment framework in a generation. The accompanying Implementing Regulations (Ministerial Decision No. 1086) were issued pursuant to the Investment Law and published in the Official Gazette on 25 April 2025, approximately ten weeks after the Law's entry into force on 12 February 2025. MISA continued to process registrations during the transitional period pending the Implementing Regulations' publication.

This guide provides a high-level executive overview of the key legal and regulatory considerations for foreign investors seeking to establish a presence in Saudi Arabia. The guide addresses five principal areas:

- The new MISA registration regime that replaced the prior licensing system
- Restricted and prohibited sectors for foreign investment
- The taxation and Zakat framework
- Labor law and Saudization (Nitaqat) requirements
- Dispute resolution and arbitration mechanisms

# 2\. The New MISA Registration Regime

The cornerstone of Saudi Arabia's reformed investment framework is the replacement of the Foreign Investment License (FIL) - historically known as the SAGIA license or MISA license - with a streamlined registration-based system administered by the Ministry of Investment (MISA). This shift reflects a fundamental philosophical change: foreign investment is now treated as a right rather than a privilege granted by ministerial discretion.

## 2.1 From Licensing to Registration: The Key Shift

Under the prior regime, foreign investors were required to obtain a sector-specific Foreign Investment License before undertaking any business activity or incorporating a company in Saudi Arabia. This meant that a company operating across multiple sectors had to obtain separate licenses for each area of activity, resulting in significant administrative burden, cost, and delay.

Under the new Investment Law, this licensing requirement has been abolished for most activities. Foreign investors must instead register with MISA through the National Investor Register before commencing any investment activity. A single MISA Registration can cover activities across multiple sectors, eliminating the need for separate approvals per activity. Registration is available digitally through the MISA e-Services Portal and is described as a significantly faster and less discretionary process than the former licensing procedure.

## 2.2 Eligibility and Pre-Conditions

Before submitting an application to MISA, a prospective investor should verify the following:

- Confirm that the intended business activity does not appear on MISA's List of Excluded Activities (see Section 3 for details)
- Determine the appropriate legal structure for the investment (LLC, branch of a foreign company, joint-stock company, simplified joint stock company or other permitted form)
- Ensure the foreign entity has been registered in its home jurisdiction for at least one year (required for certain license types, including service company registrations)
- Verify whether sector-specific approvals from other ministries or regulators are required prior to or alongside the MISA registration (e.g., industrial activities require approval from the Ministry of Industry and Mineral Resources)

## 2.3 Documentation Requirements

The documentation required varies depending on the type of investor (legal entity or natural person) and the nature of the investment. The following table summarizes the core documentary requirements for a foreign legal entity:

| **Document** | **Notes** |
| --- | --- |
| Certificate of Incorporation / Commercial Registration from home country | Must be notarized and authenticated by the Saudi Embassy in the home country; Apostille Seal required for Hague Convention signatories |
| Articles of Association / Memorandum of Incorporation | Must outline corporate structure, shareholder rights, and governance framework; must align with Saudi Companies Law requirements |
| Audited financial statements (most recent fiscal year) | Demonstrates financial standing and capital adequacy; must be legalized up to the Saudi Embassy in the home country.; Apostille Seal required for Hague Convention signatories. |
| Shareholder / ownership structure details | Full disclosure of beneficial ownership and capital contributions required. |
| Board resolution authorizing Saudi investment | Authorizing the establishment and designating a local representative. |
| Power of attorney for local representative | Grants authority to act on behalf of the company in the Kingdom. |
| Arabic translations of all documents | Mandatory; all documents must be rendered in Arabic prior to submission |

All documents must be notarized, legalized, and attested. For Hague Convention member states, an Apostille Seal replaces the need for Saudi Embassy legalization. Documents in languages other than Arabic must be accompanied by certified Arabic translations. In practice, Articles of Association follow a template predetermined by the Ministry of Commerce, uploaded on the Saudi Business Center's (SBC) e-portal with a drop-down menu of powers and governance provisions. Investors should familiarize themselves with this template at an early stage. The board resolution requirement is of relevance in the case of a branch of a foreign entity, where the authorizing resolution must clearly empower the branch to operate in the Kingdom. In addition, an Ultimate Beneficial Ownership (UBO) declaration requirement has been introduced, which must be complied with on an annual basis concomitantly with the confirmation of the Commercial Register (CR) certificate. This is a live compliance obligation and must be factored into post-registration planning.

## 2.4 Minimum Capital Requirements

Minimum capital requirements vary by license type and business activity. There is no single universal minimum applicable across all sectors. The following indicative thresholds apply under current MISA guidelines:

| **Business / License Type** | **Minimum Capital Requirement** |
| --- | --- |
| General service license (LLC or branch) | SAR 25,000 (approx. USD 6,700). Notwithstanding this official minimum capital requirement, we recommend setting the capital at SAR. 100,000\*. |
| Professional services (consulting, legal, engineering, architecture) - 100% foreign-owned | Minimum capital requirement of SAR 10 million per KSA branch (consistent with Section 2.5); requires offices in at least 4 countries |
| Professional services - joint venture (min. 25% Saudi stake) | Minimum capital requirement similar to service activities (we recommend setting the capital at SAR.100,000); Saudi partnership exempts global presence requirement |
| Commercial / trading license (100% foreign-owned) | SAR 30 million (approx. USD 8 million); 3+ country operations; SAR 200-300 million investment commitment over 5 years |
| Commercial / trading license - joint venture (min. 25% Saudi stake) | SAR 26.6 million (approx. USD 7 million); no global operations requirement |
| Industrial / manufacturing license | Capital requirements mirror service license (we recommend setting the capital at SAR. 100,000\*); sector-specific approvals required \[NOTE: Capital requirements for industrial/manufacturing licenses must be independently verified against current MISA and Ministry of Industry and Mineral Resources (MIM) guidelines before publication. Requirements may be determined on a case-by-case or sector-specific basis.\] |
| Entrepreneur / startup license | Evaluated on a case-by-case basis; venture capital support or endorsement from approved bodies (e.g., KAUST) required |
| Branch of a foreign company | SAR 25,000 minimum; branch name must be unique and derived from parent company name |

\*These capital requirements are subject to frequent changes and where the MISA guide provides that the minimum capital mirrors that of service activities, it is recommended to set the capital at SAR. 100,000. In practice, the minimum paid-up capital may be withdrawn once the Commercial Register (CR) certificate has been issued, subject to the company's own working capital needs.

## 2.5 Mandatory Saudi Participation in Certain Activities

While the new Investment Law permits 100% foreign ownership across most sectors, a number of activities continue to require either mandatory Saudi partnership or impose conditions designed to achieve a level of local participation:

- Professional services firms (law, engineering, architecture, consulting): full foreign ownership is permitted only where the firm has a presence in at least four countries and meets the SAR 10 million per branch capital requirement. Alternatively, a joint venture with at least 25% Saudi participation is permitted at lower capital thresholds.
- Commercial / trading activities: full foreign ownership is subject to stringent capital and operational conditions; joint ventures with 25% Saudi participation are an alternative route.
- Certain regulated activities (e.g., banking, insurance, healthcare, media) remain subject to sector-specific regulations that may impose ownership restrictions or require approval from the relevant sectoral regulator.
- Activities on the List of Excluded Activities may be approved by MISA on a case-by-case basis and may require a Saudi joint venture partner.

Separately, as of 2024, any company wishing to bid on Saudi government contracts or access government services must establish its regional headquarters within the Kingdom. This requirement - promoted by MISA in partnership with the Royal Commission for Riyadh City - effectively mandates a substantive local presence for multinationals seeking public-sector business.

## 2.6 Post-Registration Steps

MISA registration is the first but not the sole requirement for commencing operations. Following registration, investors must:

- Obtain a Commercial Registration (CR) from the Ministry of Commerce - the legal "birth certificate" of the company in Saudi Arabia
- Register a trade name with the Ministry of Commerce online portal
- Draft and execute Articles of Association
- Register with ZATCA (Zakat, Tax and Customs Authority) for tax and VAT compliance
- Register with GOSI (General Organization for Social Insurance) for employment and social insurance
- Obtain a municipality permit and any sector-specific licenses or approvals
- Open a local corporate bank account

The Saudi Business Centers (SBC) provide a one-stop interface for many of these steps. It is also worth noting that MISA operates Investor Relations Centers that offer post-registration support services.

# 3\. Restricted and Prohibited Sectors for Foreign Investors

The new Investment Law replaces the prior "Negative List" with a List of Excluded Activities, which catalogues sectors in which foreign investment is prohibited or restricted. The key conceptual shift from the prior framework is that the default position is now one of openness: foreign investors may invest in any sector or activity not appearing on the Excluded Activities list, rather than requiring express authorization for permitted activities.

## 3.1 The List of Excluded Activities

The List of Excluded Activities is developed and updated by a ministerial committee and published by MISA. The current list continues to restrict or prohibit foreign investment in the following principal sectors:

| **Excluded / Restricted Sector** | **Status** |
| --- | --- |
| Exploration, prospecting, and production of petroleum substances (excluding certain mining-related services) | Generally prohibited for foreign investors; operated by Saudi Aramco and JV partners under specific concession agreements |
| Manufacturing of military equipment, uniforms, and devices | Prohibited; defense production reserved for national entities |
| Security and investigation services | Restricted; Saudi national participation required |
| Real estate brokerage | Restricted; subject to specific regulatory conditions |
| Retail / wholesale of certain commodities (certain sub-categories) | Subject to licensing conditions; some categories remain restricted |
| Broadcasting and satellite media services | Regulated; subject to Communications and Media Commission approval |
| Hajj and Umrah services | Restricted to Saudi entities in certain sub-sectors |
| Certain fishing and maritime activities | Subject to specific conditions |
| Activities posing national security concerns (as determined by MISA's Screening Committee) | Subject to case-by-case review and potential prohibition |

The full and current List of Excluded Activities is published by MISA and updated periodically in the MISA Investor Guide. Investors are strongly advised to conduct a formal activity check against the current list before proceeding.

## 3.2 Seeking Approval for Excluded Activities

Where a foreign investor wishes to engage in an Excluded Activity, the new Investment Law provides a mechanism to apply for prior approval from MISA. Applications are reviewed by MISA and a Screening Committee, which may request additional information. MISA reserves the right to suspend a foreign investment for national security reasons.

This is an important departure from the prior framework under which the Council of Ministers approved amendments to the Negative List, while SAGIA (now MISA) administered individual applications in coordination with relevant ministries. The new framework centralizes both functions within MISA and its Screening Committee, providing a clearer ministry-level pathway for exceptional approvals. The process remains discretionary and subject to national interest considerations.

## 3.3 Special Economic Zones (SEZs)

Saudi Arabia operates several Special Economic Zones that offer tailored regulatory environments, including modified ownership rules, customs facilitations, and sector-specific incentives. Notable SEZs include the King Abdullah Economic City (KAEC) SEZ, the Jazan SEZ (industrial and logistics), the Ras Al-Khair SEZ (mining and metals), and the Cloud Computing Special Zone. Distinct from the SEZ framework, major megaprojects including NEOM, the Red Sea Project, and Diriyah operate under their own bespoke special regulatory frameworks. The new Investment Law expressly states that its protections apply to investors within SEZs, without prejudice to the specific legislation governing each zone. Investors considering an SEZ-based entry strategy should review the applicable zone-specific regulations alongside the general investment framework.

**4.** **Taxation and Zakat Framework**

Saudi Arabia operates a dual fiscal system for businesses: Corporate Income Tax (CIT) applies to the foreign-owned portion of any enterprise, while Zakat - an Islamic wealth levy - applies to the Saudi and GCC-owned portions. Both are administered by the Zakat, Tax and Customs Authority (ZATCA). Importantly, Saudi Arabia does not impose personal income tax on salaries or wages of any individual, whether Saudi national or expatriate.

## 4.1 Corporate Income Tax (CIT)

The standard CIT rate is 20% on the net adjusted taxable profits of a foreign-owned entity. The tax applies to:

- Branches of foreign companies (taxed on the same basis as locally incorporated entities)
- Foreign-owned subsidiaries incorporated in Saudi Arabia
- The foreign ownership portion of joint ventures (the Saudi portion is subject to Zakat, not CIT)

Special rates apply in the hydrocarbons sector: income from oil and hydrocarbon production is subject to tax at rates ranging from 50% to 85% depending on capital investment levels and cost recovery periods. Natural gas investment is taxed under the general provisions of the income tax legislation (i.e., at 20%) following the repeal of the Natural Gas Investment Tax in 2018 (Royal Decree No. M/7, dated 2/2/1440H).

Annual CIT returns must be filed within 120 days of the end of the financial year. Ordinary business expenses, including salaries, rent, and depreciation, are deductible before calculating the taxable base.

## 4.2 Zakat

Zakat is an Islamic wealth levy assessed on Saudi and GCC-owned entities and on the Saudi/GCC ownership portion of mixed-ownership companies. Unlike CIT, Zakat is not based on profits alone but is assessed on the entity's Zakat base, calculated using an indirect method that begins with the company's accounting equity and adjusts for specific items to arrive at a working capital proxy representing zakatable net worth. The standard Zakat rate is 2.5% of the zakatable base.

New Zakat Regulations issued by ZATCA took effect on 1 January 2024, standardizing and modernizing the classification of assets and liabilities for Zakat purposes. Companies with significant assets may be able to reduce their Zakat base through permissible deductions such as long-term liabilities.

For mixed-ownership companies (combining Saudi/GCC and foreign shareholders), both CIT and Zakat will apply, allocated proportionally to the respective ownership stakes. Careful planning of ownership structure is therefore a critical element of tax optimization for joint ventures.

## 4.3 Value Added Tax (VAT)

Saudi Arabia imposes VAT at a standard rate of 15% on most goods and services. This is among the higher VAT rates in the GCC region. Key compliance points include:

- Businesses must register for VAT with ZATCA once taxable turnover exceeds ZATCA's registration threshold of SAR 375,000 per annum (mandatory registration threshold). Voluntary VAT registration is available from SAR 187,500 per annum
- VAT returns must be filed and remitted to ZATCA on a quarterly basis for businesses with annual taxable supplies below SAR 40 million, and on a monthly basis for businesses at or above that threshold
- E-invoicing (Fatoora) is mandatory for all businesses; invoices must meet ZATCA specifications or face penalties
- Certain sectors benefit from zero-rating or exemption, including healthcare, education, and direct exports
- April 2025 amendments to the VAT Implementing Regulations introduced VAT grouping flexibility for multinational companies and new digital marketplace obligations for e-commerce platforms

## 4.4 Withholding Tax (WHT)

Withholding tax applies to certain payments made by Saudi-resident entities to non-resident persons. Key WHT rates are:

| **Payment Type** | **Standard WHT Rate** |
| --- | --- |
| Dividends paid to non-residents | 5%  |
| Technical and management fees | 15% |
| Royalties | 15% |
| Rent (non-real estate) | 15% |
| Insurance and re-insurance premiums | 5%  |
| International air and sea freight | 5%  |
| Other services rendered by non-residents | 15% |

WHT must be remitted within 10 days of the month following the payment. Failure to withhold and remit constitutes a violation that may attract penalties.

## 4.5 Double Taxation Treaties (DTTs)

Saudi Arabia has signed over 50 double taxation treaties with other countries, providing foreign investors from treaty partner states with the ability to reduce applicable WHT rates - in some cases to zero. Countries with favorable DTT arrangements include the United Arab Emirates, the United Kingdom, and the United States, among many others. Investors should consult qualified tax advisors to assess treaty eligibility and structuring opportunities.

## 4.6 Tax Incentives

Two significant tax incentive programs are available to qualifying foreign investors:

- Regional Headquarters (RHQ) Tax Holiday: Companies that establish their regional headquarters in Saudi Arabia may qualify for a 30-year exemption from CIT on qualifying income and a 0% WHT rate on payments to non-residents (dividends, management fees, and service fees), provided specific economic substance criteria are met. The exemption applies only to qualifying income directly attributable to the RHQ's licensed activities in the region, subject to economic substance compliance as defined under Ministerial Resolution No. (1528) of 1444H. Income from activities outside the RHQ's defined scope and payments not meeting the economic substance requirements remain subject to standard CIT and WHT rates. This incentive has been a major driver of multinational investment in Riyadh.
- Special Economic Zone (SEZ) Incentives: Businesses operating within designated SEZs may benefit from reduced customs duties, favorable regulatory treatment, and sector-specific fiscal concessions.
- Regional Development Incentives: Companies operating in designated development regions (including Ha'il, Jazan, Najran, Al-Baha, Al-Jouf, and the Northern Territory) may claim deductions on training costs and Saudi employee salaries.

## 4.7 Transfer Pricing

Saudi Arabia has adopted transfer pricing rules aligned with OECD guidelines. Related-party transactions exceeding SAR 6 million require documentation in the form of master and local files. Routine services are typically priced using the cost-plus method at a 5-10% margin. ZATCA conducts recurring tax audits, and audit preparedness - including maintaining records for a minimum of 10 years for income tax purposes and 6 years for VAT purposes (per the VAT Implementing Regulations as amended April 2025 and ZATCA's audit guidelines) is a critical compliance requirement. Multinational groups with consolidated annual revenues of SAR 3.2 billion or more (approximately USD 853 million) are additionally subject to Country-by-Country Reporting (CbCR) obligations, requiring both a CbCR notification and an annual CbCR report to be filed with ZATCA. Investors in this category should refer to ZATCA's Transfer Pricing Guidelines for filing timelines and format requirements.

# 5\. Labor Law and Saudization (Nitaqat) Requirements

Saudi Arabia's labor market is governed by the Labor Law (Royal Decree M/51) and administered by the Ministry of Human Resources and Social Development (MHRSD). A defining feature of the regulatory environment for all employers - whether Saudi or foreign - is the Saudization policy, formally implemented through the Nitaqat program.

## 5.1 Overview of the Nitaqat Classification System

Saudization (Nitaqat) requires private sector employers to maintain a minimum percentage of Saudi national employees as a proportion of their total workforce. The required percentage varies by industry sector, company size, and the number of professionals in certain specialist roles. Companies are assigned a color-coded Nitaqat category that determines their operational privileges:

| **Nitaqat Category** | **Status and Implications** |
| --- | --- |
| Platinum | Highest Saudization level; maximum privileges including fast-track visa processing, unrestricted employee transfers, priority government services |
| High Green | Compliant; broad flexibility in visa management, employee transfers, and profession changes |
| Mid Green | Compliant; standard operational flexibility; recommended minimum target for foreign companies |
| Low Green | Partially compliant; limited benefits; some restrictions on government services |
| Red | Non-compliant; severe restrictions including prohibition on new work permit issuance, visa bans, financial penalties, and risk of commercial registration non-renewal |

## 5.2 Saudization Quotas

The required Saudization percentage depends on company size and sector. General guidance (subject to sector-specific variations) is as follows:

- Small companies (up to 5 employees): at least 1 Saudi national must be employed
- Medium-sized companies (6-100 employees): Saudization percentage varies by sector
- Large companies (100+ employees): minimum Saudization rate of 30%, subject to sector-specific higher requirements

In addition to overall quota thresholds, certain roles are reserved exclusively for Saudi nationals, including Senior HR Manager, Recruitment Specialist, Government Relations Officer, Director of Labor Affairs, and Personnel Manager.

The minimum monthly salary for a Saudi national to count toward the Saudization quota is SAR 4,000 (previously SAR 3,000). For certain specialist professions, such as engineering, the minimum is higher (SAR 7,000). A Saudi employee earning below the threshold counts only as 0.5 persons toward the quota. Employing a person with a recognized disability counts as 4 persons toward the quota, provided the employee is registered with the Ministry of Human Resources and holds a valid disability certificate from the relevant authority (e.g., HRDF or Ministry of Health). Investors should verify the current multiplier against the most recent MHRSD Nitaqat program circular, as this figure has been subject to periodic review.

## 5.3 Sector-Specific Saudization Updates (2025)

As part of Saudi Arabia's ongoing expansion of Saudization obligations, several key sectors are subject to updated and escalating requirements in 2025:

| **Sector** | **2025 Saudization Requirements** |
| --- | --- |
| Engineering (5+ engineers) | 30% Saudization requirement effective 27 July 2025 |
| Accounting (5+ accountants) | 40% effective 27 October 2025, increasing by 10% annually through 2028 (to reach 70%) |
| Dentistry (3+ dental professionals) | 45% from 27 July 2025, rising to 55% by 27 January 2026 |
| Hospitals | 65% from 27 July 2025 |
| Community pharmacies | 35% from 27 July 2025 |
| Other pharmacy businesses | 55% from 27 July 2025 |
| Consulting firms | 40% for financial consulting specialists, business consultants, and project management directors |

## 5.4 Practical Compliance Considerations

Foreign investors should build Saudization compliance into their business plans from inception. Key practical points include:

- Saudization status is automatically calculated and monitored through the Qiwa platform maintained by MHRSD. Employers must ensure that all employment contracts and changes are registered promptly on the platform, as Nitaqat classifications are recalculated based on live data
- As of April 2024, foreign natural persons who personally own and operate a private establishment (مؤسسة فردية) registered in their own name in Saudi Arabia are counted as Saudi nationals for Nitaqat quota purposes. This provision does not extend to foreign-owned LLCs or other corporate entities - only to individually-owned private establishments registered directly in the foreign natural person's name
- GCC nationals are treated the same as Saudi nationals for Saudization quota purposes
- Part-time and flexible employees (minimum 160 hours per month) now count as a full Nitaqat point, increasing workforce planning flexibility
- Companies in the Red category face restrictions on work permit renewals, the right to hire new expatriates, and access to government platforms - a situation that can rapidly impair operations
- Obtaining a Saudization certificate (through the Etimad platform) is mandatory for companies seeking to bid on public sector and government projects
- Non-compliance can result in monetary fines, reputational damage, and operational restrictions including potential non-renewal of the Commercial Registration

**6\. Dispute Resolution and Arbitration**

Saudi Arabia has made significant strides in developing its dispute resolution framework, with the new Investment Law providing clear legal recognition of alternative dispute resolution mechanisms for the first time in the Kingdom's investment legislation.

## 6.1 Investor Rights and Protections

The new Investment Law (Article 10) enshrines key protections for all investors, providing the foundational basis for dispute resolution:

- Investments may not be confiscated or expropriated except pursuant to a final judicial ruling, in accordance with statutory procedures, and with payment of fair compensation
- Investors are entitled to fair and equitable treatment and are protected from denial of justice and arbitrary or discriminatory measures
- Investors have the freedom to repatriate investment proceeds, profits, dividends, and capital
- All investors - local and foreign - are to be treated equally under comparable circumstances

## 6.2 Courts and Alternative Dispute Resolution

The Investment Law establishes a tiered dispute resolution framework:

Competent Courts: Disputes between investors and the competent authority (i.e., MISA or other government bodies) are to be resolved by the Saudi competent courts as the default mechanism, unless the parties agree otherwise.

Alternative Dispute Resolution (ADR): The Investment Law expressly permits investors to agree to resolve disputes through ADR mechanisms, including:

- Arbitration
- Mediation
- Conciliation

This is a landmark development: under the prior Foreign Investment Law of 2000, the only recognized mechanism for investor-government disputes was amicable negotiation. The new law explicitly expands options to include arbitration and other ADR methods, subject to agreement between the parties. Investor-to-investor disputes may also be resolved through ADR.

## 6.3 Arbitration in Saudi Arabia

Domestic arbitration in Saudi Arabia is governed by the Arbitration Law (Royal Decree No. M/34, 1433H / 2012), which was modeled on the UNCITRAL Model Law and has positioned the Kingdom as a more arbitration-friendly jurisdiction than it was historically. Key features of the current framework include:

- Saudi governmental bodies and state-owned companies may refer disputes with foreign investors to arbitration without Prime Ministerial approval in certain circumstances, provided the arbitration is conducted at the Saudi Center for Commercial Arbitration (SCCA) or another licensed center
- For contracts exceeding SAR 100 million in value, ministerial approval and the application of Saudi law are standard conditions for government-entity arbitration. However, the ministerial approval requirement has been progressively relaxed under recent reforms, and certain government bodies may now enter arbitration agreements at the SCCA without prior ministerial approval. Legal advice should be sought on the applicable requirements for specific government counterparties
- The SCCA has published updated arbitration rules that recognize emergency arbitration, allowing parties to seek urgent interim relief
- Saudi arbitral awards are binding and final, with grounds for annulment limited to violations of Sharia, public policy, improper tribunal formation, or party incapacity.

## 6.4 International Arbitration

Saudi Arabia is a signatory to the ICSID Convention and has ratified a number of bilateral investment treaties (BITs), providing foreign investors from treaty partner states with access to investor-state arbitration under international rules. Importantly, Saudi Arabia submitted a notification under Article 25(4) of the ICSID Convention limiting the categories of disputes eligible for ICSID arbitration, including certain restrictions applicable to petroleum-related disputes. Saudi Arabia's BIT network, while growing, is not uniformly active, and several treaties remain unratified or subject to reservations. Investors should verify treaty coverage and ICSID eligibility on a transaction-specific basis before relying on international arbitration protections. Saudi Arabia's BITs typically include most-favored-nation clauses and protections for indirect investments.

International arbitration bodies may be used for contracts with foreign parties where specific criteria are met, including ministerial approval for government-entity involvement and the inclusion of the arbitration clause in contract documents.

## 6.5 The Draft New Arbitration Law

As of late 2025, Saudi Arabia published a Draft Arbitration Law for public consultation, with the consultation period having closed on 24 October 2025. Key proposed improvements include:

- Explicit recognition of emergency arbitrators for urgent interim relief
- Authorization for virtual hearings through technology platforms, reducing cost and logistical barriers for cross-border disputes
- Broader definition of written arbitration agreement, encompassing electronic communications, correspondence, and digital signatures
- The ability to issue interim and conservatory measures by arbitral tribunals, enforceable by the courts
- Finality of arbitral awards with narrow, clearly defined grounds for annulment

These developments are expected to further enhance Saudi Arabia's profile as a viable seat for high-value international commercial arbitration, complementing the broader reforms designed to attract foreign direct investment.

**7\. Conclusion**

Saudi Arabia's legal and regulatory landscape for foreign investment has been transformed by a generation's worth of reform compressed into a few years. The new Investment Law of 2025, combined with the reformed Companies Law, the Civil Transactions Law, the new Personal Data Protection Law, and evolving labor and tax regulations, collectively create one of the most investor-friendly frameworks in the region.

For prospective foreign investors, the key takeaways from this guide are:

- **MISA Registration is now the gateway to investment in Saudi Arabia, replacing the prior licensing regime with a faster, more transparent, and less discretionary process. A single registration covers multiple sectors, and the system treats foreign investment as a right for activities not on the Excluded Activities list.**
- **Sector openness has expanded significantly. While a List of Excluded Activities remains (principally covering petroleum production, defense manufacturing, and a handful of other strategic areas), the vast majority of sectors are now open to 100% foreign ownership.**
- **The tax framework is predictable: a 20% CIT on foreign-owned entities, 2.5% Zakat on Saudi/GCC-owned portions, 15% VAT, and a withholding tax regime mitigated by an extensive network of double taxation treaties. The RHQ tax holiday program offers particularly compelling incentives for multinationals willing to establish a substantive regional presence.**
- **Saudization compliance is non-negotiable and increasingly sector specific. Foreign companies must integrate Nitaqat workforce planning into their operational strategy from day one, as non-compliance carries serious operational and reputational consequences.**
- **Dispute resolution options have expanded materially. The new Investment Law's recognition of arbitration and mediation for investor-government disputes, combined with a modernizing arbitration law, provides foreign investors with greater confidence in the enforceability of their rights.**

The pace of regulatory change in Saudi Arabia remains high. Investors are strongly encouraged to engage qualified legal and regulatory counsel on the ground in the Kingdom to navigate the evolving landscape and ensure full compliance with all applicable requirements.
